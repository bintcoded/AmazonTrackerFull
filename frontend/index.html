About the Amazon

The Lungs of Our Planet

The Amazon Rainforest is the largest tropical rainforest on Earth, spanning over 5.5 million km² across nine countries and covering nearly 40% of South America. Home to more than 10% of all species on Earth, it hosts over 40,000 plant species, 3,000 freshwater fish, 1,300 bird species, and 430 mammals.

The Amazon produces approximately 20% of the world's oxygen and stores an estimated 150–200 billion tonnes of carbon, making it the single most important terrestrial buffer against climate change.

Since 1970, over 800,000 km² of forest have been lost to deforestation. Scientists warn that if deforestation reaches 20–25% of total forest loss, the Amazon could begin converting irreversibly into dry savanna while releasing catastrophic amounts of carbon and triggering climate feedback loops felt worldwide.
